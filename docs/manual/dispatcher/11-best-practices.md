# Dispatcher Best Practices

The unwritten rules. What separates good dispatchers from great ones.

## Communication

**Be responsive.** Drivers and brokers expect same-day responses. 30 minutes is normal during business hours; an hour is the upper bound. Longer than that, you owe an explanation.

**Be proactive.** Don't wait for the broker to ask "where's my load?" — text them an update when you have one. A quick "driver is rolling, ETA on track" prevents a phone call.

**Be brief but complete.** Long messages tire out drivers. Short messages without context confuse them. Find the middle.

**Use names.** "Hey Marcus" beats "Hey Driver." Brokers and drivers notice when you remember them.

**Use Load IDs.** "LD-3492" is more precise than "the Chicago load." Always include it when there's any ambiguity.

## Assignment

**Match drivers to loads thoughtfully.** Location, equipment, HOS, driver preference — all matter. The dropdown shows you available drivers; you choose.

**Don't over-assign one driver.** Spreading loads across the fleet keeps everyone happy. A driver who feels they get the worst loads will quit.

**Honor driver preferences.** If a driver tells you "I don't want overnight loads," try to respect that. The fleet has flexibility; use it.

**Assign with context.** When you assign a non-routine load, send a quick message: "Got a 600-mile pickup tomorrow morning, you good?" Better than dispatching blind.

## Status discipline

**Verify before manually advancing.** Auto-update is usually right. Manual updates should be reserved for clear cases (driver phone confirmation, geofence miss).

**Don't game the status.** Advancing to "Delivered" because the broker is impatient and you want them off your back is dishonest. The status tracker is a record; tell the truth.

**Correct quickly when wrong.** A misfired status that you notice should be corrected within minutes, not hours. The customer tracker shows the status; stale wrong data damages credibility.

## Working with drivers

**Listen first.** When a driver calls with a problem, hear them out before jumping to solutions. They often have context you don't.

**Don't argue about HOS.** If a driver says they don't have hours, they don't have hours. Plan around it. Pressuring drivers to violate HOS is illegal and unethical.

**Respect home time.** Drivers have lives. If a driver tells you they're going home Friday, don't try to slip a Saturday delivery onto their schedule.

**Don't single out underperformers in front of others.** Drivers talk. If you're addressing performance, do it privately.

**Recognize good work.** A simple "great job on that delivery" message takes 5 seconds and lasts in the driver's head for days.

## Working with brokers

**Set expectations early.** When you book a load, confirm the delivery window in writing (email or rate con). Misunderstandings about times are the most common source of broker disputes.

**Send tracking links proactively.** It cuts the "where is my load?" calls by 80%.

**Don't accept rate cons you can't fulfill.** If you don't have an available driver for a load, decline upfront. Accepting and then scrambling is worse than declining.

**Be honest about delays.** "Driver hit construction, 30 min late" goes over better than silence followed by a missed appointment.

**Don't badmouth other carriers.** Even if a broker is venting about a competitor. Stay professional.

## Daily habits

**Morning glance.** First thing: Job Board → Notifications → Tracking map. 5 minutes total.

**Mid-day check-in.** Around 11 AM and 2 PM, scan the dashboard. Address anything new.

**End-of-day scan.** Before clocking out: every active load has a current status, every recent delivery has a POD uploaded, no driver is dangling without instructions.

**Per-load discipline.** When you touch a load (assign, reassign, status update, message), close the loop: confirm the driver got the message, confirm the broker is informed if relevant.

## Documentation

**Note unusual things.** If a delivery had a problem (damage, refused load, late arrival), make a brief note in the load's messages or notifications. Future-you (or your replacement) will thank you.

**Use Load IDs consistently.** In emails, in texts, in phone calls — always reference Load IDs. Reduces confusion.

**Keep BOL photos.** Even after the driver uploads POD, brokers sometimes ask for "the signed BOL." Have it findable.

**Don't rely on memory.** A small set of running mental notes (which drivers prefer what kinds of loads, which brokers are slow payers, which receivers are notoriously slow) is fine. A bigger set should be written down.

## Decision-making

**Defer to drivers on their own equipment and hours.** They know their truck. They know their body. Don't override their judgment.

**Defer to brokers on the rate.** It was agreed at booking. Mid-load renegotiation rarely works and damages relationships.

**Defer to Super Admin on policy.** When in doubt about whether something is allowed (a specific cancellation reason, an unusual expense, a borderline driver behavior), ask. Better to confirm than to act and apologize.

**Use your own judgment on operational details.** Where to message vs. call. How to phrase a status update. Which driver gets a competitive load. That's your craft.

## When things go wrong

**Stay calm.** Drivers and brokers will mirror your energy. A panicked dispatcher creates more panic.

**Triage.** Of the things going wrong right now, which actually need attention in the next 30 minutes? Handle those first.

**Document.** Even in the heat of the moment, log key facts. Memory blurs; written records don't.

**Escalate appropriately.** Some problems need Super Admin. Some need the broker. Some need leadership. Know who to call and call them.

**Don't blame.** When recapping a problem, focus on what happened and what to do next. Not who's at fault. Blame games burn bridges.

## After hours

**Hand off cleanly.** If after-hours coverage exists, tell them what's outstanding before you go off duty.

**Be available for emergencies.** Drivers should know how to reach you (or whoever's covering) for a true emergency.

**Don't drag the day into the night.** Long hours burn dispatchers out. Set boundaries.

## Career

**Build relationships, not just transactions.** The drivers you work with, the brokers you book with — these are long-term relationships. Treat them like it.

**Learn the patterns.** Every fleet has rhythms — busy days, slow days, broker seasons, equipment quirks. Pay attention.

**Get to know the technology.** The more you understand how the system works, the better you'll be at troubleshooting and the less time you'll spend on rote stuff.

**Talk to other dispatchers.** Whether at your company or others, peer dispatchers have stories and tricks. Listen.

## A final word

Dispatch is a job that rewards consistency. Showing up, communicating clearly, handling exceptions calmly, doing the routine stuff routinely — that's what makes a fleet run. The drivers see it. The brokers see it. Leadership sees it.

The flashy moves (saving a load that's about to go sideways) get attention. The steady stuff (every load on time, every message answered) is what builds careers.
