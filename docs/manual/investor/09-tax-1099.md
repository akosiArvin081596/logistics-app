# Tax Forms and Year-End

January brings tax season. This chapter is what to expect, what you'll receive, and what to do with it.

## What you'll receive

Depending on your structure (individual, single-member LLC, multi-member LLC, S-Corp), one of these tax forms will be generated:

### 1099-NEC

If you receive distributions as an individual or single-member LLC (treated as a sole proprietor for tax purposes), LogisX issues a **1099-NEC** documenting the total payments for the year.

The 1099-NEC shows:

- **Your name and TIN** (from the W-9 you signed during onboarding).
- **LogisX's payer information.**
- **Box 1:** total nonemployee compensation paid to you during the year.
- **Box 4:** any backup withholding (usually $0 if your W-9 is in order).

Form arrives in your Document Portal in late January for the prior tax year. You'll also typically receive a physical or PDF copy emailed.

### K-1

If your structure is a multi-member LLC or partnership with LogisX, you receive a **Schedule K-1** instead of (or in addition to) a 1099. The K-1 reports your share of the partnership's income, deductions, credits.

K-1s arrive later than 1099s (usually March, sometimes later) because the partnership's full accounting needs to close first.

### W-2

If you're an employee (rare for investors but possible in some structures), you receive a W-2. Unlikely for an investor relationship.

## How distributions are taxed

The structure matters:

**As a sole proprietor (1099-NEC):**
- All distributions are reportable income.
- Pay self-employment tax (Social Security + Medicare) on net profit.
- Deduct legitimate business expenses (fuel, repairs, insurance) on Schedule C.
- Pay quarterly estimated taxes during the year.

**As a multi-member LLC or partnership (K-1):**
- Income flows through to your personal return.
- Your share of profit, expenses, depreciation comes through the K-1.
- Pay estimated taxes on the flow-through income.

**As an S-Corp (rare):**
- Profits flow through.
- Distinctions between salary and distributions matter for self-employment tax.

Talk to a CPA. Don't guess on tax structure.

## Depreciation

A major tax benefit of truck ownership: **depreciation**.

- **Section 179** allows immediate expensing of business equipment (including trucks) up to certain limits.
- **Bonus depreciation** allows accelerated expensing.
- **MACRS** (Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System) standard depreciation schedule.

Trucks are depreciable assets. The depreciation schedule and choices affect your taxable income significantly. A new truck purchase in December can be a substantial year-end deduction.

Work with a CPA. The choices are not trivial.

## What's in your Document Portal at year-end

By the end of January for the prior tax year:

- **1099-NEC or K-1** (whichever applies).
- **Year-summary report PDF** — you can generate it via Download Report with the full-year range. Has total revenue, total expenses, all categories — useful for your CPA.

By March:

- **K-1** if applicable.

You should also save copies of all monthly reports during the year — they're the supporting detail behind the year-summary.

## What to give your CPA

Hand off:

- **1099-NEC or K-1.**
- **Year-summary report PDF.**
- **All monthly reports** for the year (12 PDFs).
- **Truck records** with purchase dates, prices, VINs.
- **Your bank statements** showing actual deposits.
- **Any documentation of capital additions** (truck additions during the year).
- **Any documentation of capital reductions** (truck sales, write-offs).

A good CPA can use all of this. A great one will ask for more.

## Common tax questions

**Q. Am I paying tax twice?**
No. The income is taxed once — to you. LogisX isn't a corporation paying corporate tax on the same dollars.

**Q. Why is my reported income on the 1099 different from what I expected?**
The 1099 shows total payments. Your "expected" might be based on dashboard earnings (which include certain accruals). Reconcile via the year-summary report.

**Q. Can I deduct truck expenses on my own return?**
Some — depends on structure. As a sole prop (1099-NEC), you can deduct ordinary and necessary business expenses including those related to your truck. Talk to CPA.

**Q. What about driver pay — is that an expense I deduct?**
Driver pay is paid by LogisX out of revenue. You see it as a deduction on your dashboard, but it's not separately a deduction on your return — it's already netted out before your distribution.

**Q. Maintenance — who pays, who deducts?**
Depends on the Master Agreement structure. In standard arrangements:
- LogisX pays for maintenance from the truck's pool of revenue.
- The maintenance expenses reduce your net profit (and thus your distribution).
- You don't separately deduct them on your return because they're already netted.

If your structure has you paying maintenance out of pocket, those are deductible separately.

**Q. Insurance premiums — same question.**
Similar pattern. LogisX pays out of truck's revenue; reduces your distribution; not separately deductible.

## Quarterly estimated taxes

If you're getting 1099 income, you owe **quarterly estimated taxes** to the IRS. Standard schedule:

- April 15: Q1 (Jan-Mar).
- June 15: Q2 (Apr-May).
- September 15: Q3 (Jun-Aug).
- January 15 of following year: Q4 (Sep-Dec).

Calculate based on your projected annual income and total tax liability. Underpaying triggers IRS penalties.

A common rule of thumb: set aside 25-30% of every distribution for taxes. Adjust based on your actual rate.

## State taxes

You also owe state income tax (in most states). Same rules — quarterly estimated payments, file annually.

If you operate across multiple states (your trucks run in different states), you may have **multi-state filing** obligations. This gets complex; CPAs handle.

## Tax planning during the year

Some moves to consider:

**Buy a truck in Q4** to use Section 179 / bonus depreciation in the current year.

**Time large expenses** (maintenance, repairs) to optimize tax year.

**Convert structure** (sole prop to LLC, LLC to S-Corp) — has tax implications. Plan.

**Retirement contributions** — SEP-IRA, Solo 401(k) allow large pre-tax contributions for self-employed.

All of these are CPA conversations. Don't DIY tax strategy at scale.

## When LogisX is the audit target

If LogisX is audited and the auditor wants records about your investment:

- LogisX will request your cooperation but won't share your personal records without permission.
- The audit may extend to investors in some cases (partnership audits especially).
- Your CPA should be involved if anything from LogisX's audit reaches you.

This is uncommon but possible. Have records organized.

## What LogisX won't do for you

- **Prepare your tax return.** Get a CPA.
- **Advise on tax strategy.** Get a CPA.
- **Provide tax legal counsel.** Get a tax attorney if questions go that deep.
- **Take responsibility for your tax compliance.** That's on you.

LogisX provides the data (1099/K-1, year-summary, monthly reports). What you do with it is between you and your tax advisor.

## Late filings and corrections

If LogisX issues a 1099 with wrong information:

1. **Compare to your records.** Is the amount actually wrong, or just unexpected?
2. **Message operations** to inquire. Walk through the discrepancy.
3. **If LogisX agrees it's wrong**, they'll issue a corrected 1099-NEC (1099-NEC Corrected). Use the corrected version on your return.

If you receive the 1099 late (after January), you can extend your personal filing deadline (form 4868). Don't ignore the late filing.

## Pro tips

- **Start tax prep in February.** Don't wait until April. CPAs are busiest in late March.
- **Reconcile your bank deposits to your 1099 / dashboard.** Numbers should agree (within timing).
- **Save digital copies of everything.** Cloud-backed.
- **Keep records for 7 years.** IRS audit window.
- **Consider an LLC structure.** Many investors find LLC structures advantageous for liability and tax planning. Talk to a lawyer and CPA.
